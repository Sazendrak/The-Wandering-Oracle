TWR DM's Guide

TWR DM's Guide​

What is this Guide?​

This page is a guide to getting started running Quests on TWR, a discord-based Westmarches campaign. Quests can be run by any player, with certain exceptions where the player may need to seek approval from staff for lore/balance reasons. As noted in our Code of Conduct, while running a quest or other content, the player acts as the DM and is considered such during play.

Preparing to DM​

Being a DM on TWR is easy and fun. Most of the adventures can be prepared in a short period of time, and you don’t have to create an entire setting and form a campaign or spend a ton of time plotting out a large overarching plot. Most Quests on TWR are run as “one-shots,” though more advanced DMs may choose to run quests in a series to tell a more intricate story.

What you Need to Run a Game​

In order to run a game as a DM on TWR, you’ll need the following:

Players​

The #quest-board channel is the perfect place to seek out players, if you do not have a full group in mind already. For traditional adventures a group of 4-6 players is recommended, but there is no specific number of players required to participate in a quest. Just be sure to balance any potential combat or roleplay encounters around the number of participants to ensure everyone gets to play.

A Plot​

Balance the three pillars of gameplay (Roleplay, Adventuring, Combat) to your liking and create a plot for your quest. It is best to keep plots simple at first until you’ve got some practice running Quests. Consider what type of story you want to tell, what type of rewards might fit with the quest, Prepare any puzzles, difficult choices, or monster statblocks for combat ahead of time so that you can spend gameplay time interacting with the players and building the story. Remember, CR values translate to what an average difficulty is for a group of 4 well balanced adventurers. Though combat should be challenging, it should still be possible to win (unless your story dictates that the party loses!).

A Format​

Some DMs prefer to run quests in a dedicated session for a more traditional experience, planning a start and end time. Others prefer a play-by-post (PBP) format, which can span multiple days or weeks and provide a more relaxed pace. Each option has benefits and detriments, so it's important to specify which one in your #quest-board post to attract the right players.

Other Tools​

Avrae​

For combat, spellcasting, and other mechanical functions it is highly recommended that you use Avrae which will automate the majority of tracking and run combat. You can also upload maps to use for tactical placement during combat.

Tupperbot​

You will want to set up at least a "Narrator Tupper" to describe scenes, effects and other miscellaneous actions during play. You may also consider creating specific Tuppers for your NPCs to improve immersion and create a more aesthetically pleasing experience.

Group Composition​

There’s no general rule that requires members of a group be of the same level. In quests that revolve around combat, we recommend restricting them by Tier as defined in the Player’s Handbook and Rules Compendium. For quests that are more roleplay-centric, this is less necessary, but bear in mind that some high level PCs may have spells and abilities that far outshine the rest of the group.

Facilitator of Fun​

You have the most important role. You guide the narrative and provide the players with an immersive and enjoyable quest. While guiding the players, it is also your responsibility to make everyone feel welcomed, creating a fun and fair environment. Here are some things to keep in mind:

You are Empowered​

Make decisions about how the group interacts with the quest; adjust or improvise your plan as needed. You have the ability to create NPCs, locations, challenges and scenarios with broad authority within your quest. Interacting with official D&D NPCs, Factions, Deities and other key lore entities may require approval from Staff; we encourage DMs who are concerned that their content may not accurately reflect the setting reach out to an official Dungeonmaster or Loremaster for guidance. See the "When to Seek Staff Approval" section below for a better understanding of when to reach out. Be aware that, in the instance of particularly challenging deviations from the setting Staff may have to retcon the contents of your quests to maintain fairness and server continuity.

Challenge your Players​

Gauge the experience level of your players, as well as what they enjoy in a game and attempt to deliver what they’re after; everyone should be able to shine. You can adjust an encounter’s difficulty by adding or removing thematically appropriate monsters but can’t create new monsters or modify them in such a way that modifies their challenge rating (aside from changing their hit points within the range afforded by their hit dice). Be careful of going too far, however. The intent is to have a collaborative and challenging story-telling experience; it’s not to do everything you can to kill your players’ characters. Similarly, destroying equipment generally shouldn’t happen unless an encounter specifically directs you to. In fact, unless they have conditions detailing their destruction (such as an ioun stone), permanent magic items can’t be destroyed.

Dealing with Disruptive Players​

If a player is acting in a disruptive manner during a play session, attempt to address the issue as soon as it becomes clear that someone (including you) might be uncomfortable. Take the player aside and explain why the behavior might be causing others to feel uncomfortable. If the problem persists, contact a Moderator or Admin for help with resolution.

Disruptive Magic Items, Spells or Class Features​

There are several magic items, spells, class features and homebrew submissions within the rules of TWR that might become disruptive to the overall enjoyment of others at the table. If use of any personal equipment, features etc. through play leads to an uncomfortable play experience, take the player aside and explain the issue. You may wish to discuss temporarily removing the item or ability from the character and providing them an alternative replacement instead. You may choose to review a character’s sheet prior to play to help identify any potential issues, You should not mandate removal of magic items or gear solely on your preferences, but rather on their impact to the story and enjoyment of the players/DM as a group.

Safety Tools​

Safety tools help ensure players aren’t pushed beyond their comfort levels. These tools let your players know you want them to have a positive experience. Some common safety tools are provided below, each with a summary of how they work. An article about one such safety kit is provided to help with further information. Ensure that you discuss these tools with your players before the game:

  • Code of Conduct: TWR has a code of conduct that outlines rules for play across the server. It defines desired and prohibited behaviors and possible consequences. Familiarize yourself with the Code of Conduct before running a quest to help understand what is acceptable and what is not.
  • Pregame, Postgame, and Out of Character Discussions: Use time before the game to explain content warnings and set boundaries. This helps you understand the players' various goals and comfort levels. Be sure to discuss the possibility of lethal combat, if applicable, as many players prefer to avoid risking the safety of their long-running PCs. During play, most DMs prefer to have an Out of Character (OOC) thread for combat and ongoing discussion of the story. After the quest, decompress and discuss what worked and what didn't with the players.
  • Be Welcoming: Welcome and encourage respectful and open conversations about issues as they arise. Show that you’re listening by avoiding defensive responses.
  • Direct Communication: Allow direct messaging between the players and yourself as the DM. Generally, do not share information sent to you in private with the rest of the group without permission.

Keep the Game Moving​

If the game gets bogged down, provide hints and clues to your players facing puzzles or engaging in combat and roleplay interactions that get frustrating. This gives players “little victories” for making good choices based on clues. Don't be afraid to skip someone's turn or allow them the ability to act retroactively in the future if one player is unavailable and disrupting the flow of play.

Running the Game​

As the DM, you are charged with not only guiding the story, but also fairly adjudicating the game’s rules and rewarding your players’ characters for their accomplishments.

The Rules of the Game​

TWR generally uses Dungeons & Dragons 5th Edition (2014) with some added Homebrew Rules. You can issue rulings to your table when the rules of the game are ambiguous or vague, but you must otherwise adhere to the rules as they are provided in the core rulebooks and can’t change them or make up your own. You must always use the most current incarnation of a rule. Oftentimes, if a rule is in dispute, you can go to the #questions channel or ping a Dungeonmaster for assistance; generally, we follow Sage Advice for clarification from the game developers and, in some cases, Homebrew or #town-hall posts will include rules clarifications. Don't be afraid to ask for help, or issue a temporary ruling while you wait to maintain the flow of play. Otherwise, the rules as written in the rulebooks always take precedence.

Quest Locks​

Depending on the content of your quest, you have the ability to "Quest Lock" PCs who are participating. Locked PCs cannot gain experience from or participate in Hunts, Trials, or any form of activity outside of your quest. They are also unable to benefit from downtime while engaged in your quest. Whether or not to lock a PC is up to you, as is whether or not their possible progression applies within your quest. Keep in mind travel, in-character duration, and mechanical balance when deciding whether or not to lock the PCs, and be sure to communicate a quest lock during your original post or pregrame discussion.

Death, Disease and Curses​

Adventuring is a risky job, and bad things often happen to adventurers. Diseases, poisons, and other similar effects persist until they’re removed, and death within a quest applies to a PC outside of the quest as well. However, thanks to the proliferation of healing and revival services from players in our setting, they are often simple to overcome. If you wish to enact permanent effects, be sure to communicate that possibility to the players during your original post or pregame discussion.

Character Rewards​

You award the players rewards for their accomplishments in the form of experience, gold, and magic items for their characters, which must be detailed in your #story-log post recording the quest.

Experience​

Players should receive experience for participating in combat and for engaging in good roleplay. DMs should follow the usual guidelines for granting Experience, and are encouraged to request approval for more than the general amount in quests which are long-running or highly impactful.

Magic Items​

Player generally expect to receive a Magic Item as a reward for their participation in your quest. It is often most exciting to include these items as possible rewards within the quest which they can then use or keep, but sometimes it is most appropriate to provide the items as a form of payment at the end. Regardless of the source, the below table outlines what rarity of items can be rewarded per PC without Staff Approval, provided that the items are available on the loot list:

TierRarity
Tier 1Uncommon
Tier 2Rare
Tier 3Very Rare
T4 and AboveLegendary
Misc. Treasure​

You may wish to award the players with gold or valuable treasures which they may keep or sell as an additional reward from the questgiver or discovery during play. The below table outlines what the maximum amount of additional treasure which can be rewarded per PC without Staff Approval. If no Magic Items are awarded, you may increase this amount by the item's trade value:

TierAmountItem Trade Value

Tier 4+100,000 gp200,000 gp

Cosmetic Rewards​

DMs may provide cosmetic rewards such as eye color changes, floating halos, or other thematically appropriate character options as they see fit, provided that they do not allow for any mechanical benefits.

When to seek Staff Approval​

As mentioned throughout the above document, there are a number of times when it is appropriate to seek approval from Staff to assist with running your quest or to ensure that your quest is in-line with the general lore and balance expectations of our server. The following list outlines the most common reasons you may need to request Staff oversight:

  • Running named NPCs, Deities or important factions from the Forgotten Realms setting
  • Providing players with information relevant to ongoing Questlines or content provided by members of staff
  • Running content inside of an area of the Forgotten Realms which a staff member is currently focused on
  • Attempting to exceed the XP, Item or Gold reward limits for long-running quests or exemplary gameplay
  • Rewarding magic items which do not appear on the existing loot lists
  • Rewarding high-level awards such as Super Legendary items, Artifacts or Boons
  • Rewarding items which provide a permanent stat boost
  • Providing other custom rewards such as automatons, legendary crafting materials, custom items, or anything that may impact future gameplay on TWR
  • Including homebrewed monsters or equipment within your quest (if included, such additions cannot leave your quest without majority Dungeonmaster approval)

DM Rewards​

Running a quest takes time and effort that we feel should be rewarded. Rewards for running a quest come in two main ways:

Omni-XP​

Omni-XP is awarded to the DM based on the average amount of XP delivered to their players. Omni-XP is restricted by tier and only applies to non-Paragon PCs.

Paragon Tokens​

Paragon Tokens can be taken in lieu of Omni-XP and can be applied to future roleplay to increase the amount of RP-XP rewarded to one of your Paragon PCs.

Please note that you MAY choose instead to have one of your PCs participate in the request and receive the same rewards as the players, instead of choosing one of the above rewards. If you do so, Staff must approve the rewards whether they fall within the otherwise stated limits or not.
